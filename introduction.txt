
INTRODUCTION

Childhood obesity represents one of the most significant public health 
challenges of the 21st century. According to the World Health Organization, 
over 390 million children and adolescents aged 5-19 years were classified 
as overweight or obese in 2022, with prevalence having quadrupled among 
adolescents since 1990. In the United States, approximately 19.7% of 
children aged 2-19 years have obesity, representing more than 14.7 million 
children and adolescents.

Racial and ethnic disparities in childhood obesity are well documented. 
Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children bear a disproportionate burden 
of obesity compared to their Non-Hispanic White counterparts. Data from 
the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) consistently 
demonstrate higher obesity prevalence among Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black 
children, driven by complex interactions between socioeconomic factors, 
dietary patterns, physical activity levels, and structural determinants 
of health including poverty and limited access to recreational facilities 
and quality food.

Early identification of children at risk for obesity is critical for 
timely intervention. Traditional screening approaches rely primarily on 
body mass index (BMI) percentile assessment at routine pediatric visits. 
However, these approaches do not leverage the full spectrum of clinical, 
behavioral, and socioeconomic data routinely collected during pediatric 
encounters. Machine learning (ML) algorithms offer the potential to 
integrate multiple data streams to generate more accurate and 
comprehensive obesity risk predictions.

Several ML approaches have been applied to childhood obesity prediction, 
including logistic regression, decision trees, Random Forest, and gradient 
boosting algorithms. These studies have reported accuracy ranges of 76-89% 
using various datasets including electronic health records and national 
survey data. However, studies specifically targeting Hispanic and 
Non-Hispanic Black pediatric populations using the most recent nationally 
representative data remain limited.

The National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) provides 
a unique opportunity to address this gap. NHANES collects nationally 
representative data on demographics, anthropometrics, dietary habits, 
physical activity, and socioeconomic factors across diverse populations, 
making it an ideal data source for developing population-specific 
obesity prediction models.

The primary objectives of this study were to:
1. Develop and compare machine learning models for predicting obesity 
   risk in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children aged 2-19 years
2. Identify the most important predictors of obesity in these populations
3. Evaluate the potential clinical utility of machine learning as a 
   screening tool for pediatric obesity in underserved communities

By focusing specifically on Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children 
using the most recent NHANES 2021-2023 data, this study addresses an 
important gap in the literature and provides actionable insights for 
pediatric clinicians and public health practitioners working with 
these underserved populations.
