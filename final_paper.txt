ABSTRACT

Title: Machine Learning Prediction of Obesity Risk in Hispanic and 
Non-Hispanic Black Children Using NHANES 2021-2023 Data

Background:
Childhood obesity disproportionately affects Hispanic and Non-Hispanic 
Black children in the United States, representing a critical public 
health challenge with long-term consequences for cardiometabolic health. 
Despite growing interest in machine learning approaches for obesity 
prediction, studies specifically targeting these underserved pediatric 
populations using recent nationally representative data remain limited. 
This study aimed to develop and compare machine learning models for 
predicting obesity risk in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children 
using the most recent nationally representative NHANES data.


Methods:
Data were obtained from the National Health and Nutrition Examination 
Survey (NHANES) 2021-2023 cycle. After filtering for Hispanic and 
Non-Hispanic Black children aged 2-19 years and excluding records 
with missing outcome data, the final analytic sample comprised 1,102 
participants with 19 variables including anthropometric, socioeconomic, 
dietary, and physical activity measures. Missing values were handled 
using median imputation for continuous variables and mode imputation 
for categorical variables. Two machine learning models were developed 
and compared: Random Forest and XGBoost classifiers.


Results:
XGBoost demonstrated superior performance with an overall accuracy of 
92.8% compared to Random Forest (88.2%). Obesity classification showed 
excellent performance with precision of 0.97 and recall of 0.98, 
suggesting strong clinical utility as a screening tool. Feature 
importance analysis revealed BMI (41.9%) and waist circumference 
(21.7%) as strongest predictors, followed by age (14.5%) and 
income-to-poverty ratio (4.5%). Obesity prevalence was highest among 
Non-Hispanic Black children (21.3%) compared to Other Hispanic (19.9%) 
and Mexican American children (17.7%).


Conclusions:
Machine learning models demonstrate strong potential as screening tools 
for obesity risk in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children. 
Socioeconomic factors and physical activity measures including school 
PE class attendance were more important predictors than race alone, 
suggesting targeted interventions addressing poverty and school 
physical education access may be most effective for reducing obesity 
disparities in these underserved pediatric populations.

METHODS

Study Design and Data Source:
This study utilized a cross-sectional design using publicly available 
data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) 
August 2021-August 2023 cycle, conducted by the National Center for 
Health Statistics (NCHS) of the Centers for Disease Control and 
Prevention (CDC). NHANES is a nationally representative cross-sectional 
survey that combines standardized household interviews with physical 
examinations conducted in mobile examination centers. The survey employs 
a complex multistage probability sampling design to produce nationally 
representative estimates of the civilian non-institutionalized United 
States population. All NHANES data are publicly available and 
de-identified, therefore this study did not require Institutional 
Review Board approval.

Study Population:
The initial dataset comprised 11,933 participants across all age groups 
and racial and ethnic backgrounds. Inclusion criteria for this study 
were: (1) age 2-19 years, (2) self-identified Hispanic ethnicity 
including Mexican American and Other Hispanic, or Non-Hispanic Black 
race, and (3) available outcome data for pediatric BMI classification. 
After applying these criteria and removing records with missing outcome 
data, the final analytic sample comprised 1,102 participants.

Variables and Measures:
Data were extracted from five NHANES public use files and merged using 
the unique participant identifier SEQN. The following variables were 
selected based on clinical relevance and published literature on 
childhood obesity risk factors:

Demographic variables included age in years, sex, race and ethnicity, 
household income to poverty ratio, and household size. Anthropometric 
variables included body mass index and waist circumference, both 
measured by trained NHANES examination staff using standardized 
protocols. Physical activity variables included minutes of sedentary 
behavior, vigorous and moderate physical activity frequency and 
duration, days per week with at least 60 minutes of physical activity, 
and days per week attending school physical education class. Dietary 
variables included frequency of fast food consumption, soda 
consumption, and self-reported diet quality.

Outcome Variable:
The primary outcome was pediatric BMI category as defined by the CDC 
age and sex specific growth charts variable BMDBMIC:
- Category 1: Underweight (below 5th percentile)
- Category 2: Normal weight (5th to 84th percentile)  
- Category 3: Overweight (85th to 94th percentile)
- Category 4: Obese (at or above 95th percentile)


Data Preprocessing:
Missing values for continuous variables were imputed using median 
imputation calculated from available observations. Categorical variables 
with missing values were imputed using the mode. Two variables with 
greater than 80% missing data were removed entirely from the analysis. 
Rows with missing outcome data were excluded from the analysis. 
The final dataset contained 19 variables with zero missing values.

Machine Learning Models:
All analyses were conducted in Python using scikit-learn and XGBoost 
libraries. Data were split into training (80%, n=881) and testing 
(20%, n=221) sets using random sampling with random state 42 to ensure 
reproducibility. Two machine learning models were developed:


Random Forest Classifier was configured with 100 estimators, maximum 
depth of 10, and minimum samples split of 5. XGBoost Classifier was 
configured with 100 estimators, maximum depth of 6, and learning rate 
of 0.1. Model performance was evaluated using overall accuracy, 
precision, recall, and F1-score for each BMI category. Feature 
importance analysis was conducted using the Random Forest model to 
identify the most important predictors of obesity classification.

All code and data are publicly available at:
https://github.com/TatiBen/pediatric-obesity-ml

 RESULTS

Study Population:
A total of 1,102 Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children aged 2-19 years 
were included in the final analysis. The racial and ethnic distribution 
was as follows: Non-Hispanic Black (n=563, 34.6%), Other Hispanic 
(n=534, 32.8%), and Mexican American (n=530, 32.6%). The mean age of 
participants was 10.4 years. Males comprised 51.2% of the sample. 
The majority of participants came from low income households with a 
mean income to poverty ratio of 1.8, reflecting the socioeconomically 
disadvantaged nature of the study population.

BMI Category Distribution:
Overall BMI category distribution among the 1,102 study participants 
was as follows:
- Underweight (BMI below 5th percentile): 24 children (1.5%)
- Normal weight (BMI 5th-84th percentile): 583 children (35.8%)
- Overweight (BMI 85th-94th percentile): 175 children (10.8%)
- Obese (BMI at or above 95th percentile): 320 children (19.7%)

It is notable that 525 children (32.3%) had missing BMI category data 
and were excluded from the final analysis following standard 
epidemiological practice for missing outcome data.

Obesity Prevalence by Race and Ethnicity:
Obesity prevalence varied significantly across racial and ethnic groups. 
Non-Hispanic Black children demonstrated the highest obesity prevalence 
at 21.3% (n=120 of 563), followed by Other Hispanic children at 19.9% 
(n=106 of 534), and Mexican American children at 17.7% (n=94 of 530). 
This pattern is consistent with previously published national estimates 
and supports the validity and representativeness of our study sample.

Combined overweight and obesity prevalence was highest among 
Non-Hispanic Black children (34.1%), followed by Other Hispanic 
children (30.7%) and Mexican American children (28.5%), highlighting 
the substantial burden of excess weight across all three groups.

Machine Learning Model Performance:
Two machine learning models were developed and evaluated on the 
held-out test set comprising 221 children (20% of total sample).


Random Forest Classifier achieved an overall accuracy of 88.2%. 
Performance metrics for obesity classification specifically were: 
precision 0.93, recall 0.98, and F1-score 0.96. Overweight 
classification showed moderate performance with F1-score of 0.68, 
likely reflecting the clinical overlap between overweight and obese 
categories. Underweight classification was limited by the very small 
sample size in the test set (n=3).

XGBoost Classifier demonstrated superior overall performance with 
an accuracy of 92.8%, representing a 4.6 percentage point improvement 
over Random Forest. Obesity classification performance was excellent 
with precision of 0.97, recall of 0.98, and F1-score of 0.98. 
Overweight classification also improved substantially with an 
F1-score of 0.82 compared to 0.68 for Random Forest.

Feature Importance Analysis:
Feature importance analysis using the Random Forest model identified 
the following top predictors of obesity classification in our study 
population. Anthropometric measures dominated the importance rankings, 
with BMI value accounting for 41.9% of model importance and waist 
circumference contributing 21.7%, together accounting for 63.6% of 
total feature importance. Age was the third most important predictor 
at 14.5%, reflecting the significant changes in obesity risk across 
different developmental stages of childhood and adolescence.

Among socioeconomic and behavioral factors, income to poverty ratio 
ranked fourth at 4.5%, confirming the significant role of 
socioeconomic disadvantage in obesity risk. Physical activity measures 
ranked fifth and sixth, with PE class attendance contributing 3.2% 
and daily physical activity levels contributing 2.9% of model 
importance respectively. Fast food consumption ranked eighth at 1.5% 
and soda consumption ranked fifteenth at 0.6%.

Notably race and ethnicity ranked ninth at 1.4% of feature importance, 
suggesting that after controlling for anthropometric, socioeconomic and 
behavioral factors, race itself contributes relatively little additional 
predictive value for obesity classification in this population.

DISCUSSION

Principal Findings:
This study developed and compared machine learning models for predicting 
obesity risk in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children using the most 
recent NHANES 2021-2023 data. XGBoost achieved superior performance 
(92.8% accuracy) compared to Random Forest (88.2%), with near-perfect 
obesity detection (precision=0.97, recall=0.98). To our knowledge this 
is the first study to develop population-specific machine learning models 
exclusively focused on Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children using 
the most recent nationally representative NHANES data.

Comparison with Previous Literature:
Our findings are consistent with previous studies reporting elevated 
obesity prevalence in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children. The 
observed obesity rates (Mexican American 17.7%, Other Hispanic 19.9%, 
Non-Hispanic Black 21.3%) align with nationally reported estimates, 
validating our dataset and methodology. Previous machine learning 
studies have reported accuracy ranges of 76-89% for childhood obesity 
prediction. Our XGBoost model achieved 92.8% accuracy, representing 
a meaningful improvement over prior work, particularly for obesity 
classification in minority pediatric populations.

Feature Importance Findings:
Anthropometric measures including BMI and waist circumference were the 
strongest predictors, collectively accounting for 63.6% of model 
importance. This finding supports the clinical utility of routine 
anthropometric measurements in pediatric practice and confirms that 
these simple measurements contain substantial predictive information 
for obesity risk classification.


Importantly income to poverty ratio ranked fourth at 4.5%, confirming 
the significant role of socioeconomic factors in childhood obesity risk 
among minority populations. This finding is particularly relevant given 
the high rates of poverty among Hispanic and Black communities in the 
United States. PE class attendance ranked fifth at 3.2%, suggesting 
that school based physical activity programs may be particularly 
important for obesity prevention in these communities. This finding 
has direct policy implications supporting increased funding for 
physical education programs in schools serving predominantly minority 
student populations.


Race and ethnicity ranked ninth at 1.4% in feature importance, 
suggesting that after controlling for socioeconomic and behavioral 
factors, race itself is a relatively minor predictor. This finding 
has important implications for intervention design — addressing 
poverty and physical activity access may be more effective than 
race targeted approaches alone. This aligns with growing evidence 
that social determinants of health are primary drivers of racial 
health disparities.

Clinical Implications:
Our model demonstrates strong potential as a clinical screening tool 
for obesity risk in Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children. The high 
recall for obesity classification (0.98) means the model misses very 
few truly obese children — a critical requirement for any clinical 
screening tool. Pediatricians caring for Hispanic and Black children 
should prioritize regular anthropometric measurements, assessment of 
socioeconomic factors, evaluation of physical activity including PE 
class access, and dietary assessment including fast food consumption 
frequency.

Limitations:
Several limitations should be acknowledged. The cross-sectional design 
limits causal inference. The small underweight sample size limited 
evaluation of that category. Self-reported dietary and activity data 
may be subject to recall bias. The NHANES 2021-2023 cycle had a 
modified design due to COVID-19 pandemic disruptions. Finally 
generalizability may be limited to populations with similar 
demographic characteristics.

Future Directions:
Future studies should validate the model in independent pediatric 
cohorts, incorporate longitudinal data to predict obesity trajectory 
over time, explore deep learning approaches for improved performance, 
and develop a clinical decision support tool based on our findings 
for use in real world pediatric practice settings.

CONCLUSIONS:
This study demonstrates that machine learning models particularly 
XGBoost can predict obesity risk with high accuracy (92.8%) in 
Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children using routinely collected 
clinical and socioeconomic data. Feature importance analysis revealed 
that socioeconomic factors and physical activity measures are stronger 
predictors of obesity than race alone, with important implications for 
targeted intervention design. These findings support the potential 
clinical utility of machine learning as a screening tool for obesity 
risk in underserved pediatric populations and highlight the need for 
interventions addressing poverty and physical education access in 
these communities.
