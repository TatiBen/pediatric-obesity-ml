
 RESULTS

Study Population:
A total of 1,102 Hispanic and Non-Hispanic Black children aged 2-19 years 
were included in the final analysis. The racial and ethnic distribution 
was as follows: Non-Hispanic Black (n=563, 34.6%), Other Hispanic 
(n=534, 32.8%), and Mexican American (n=530, 32.6%). The mean age of 
participants was 10.4 years. Males comprised 51.2% of the sample. 
The majority of participants came from low income households with a 
mean income to poverty ratio of 1.8, reflecting the socioeconomically 
disadvantaged nature of the study population.

BMI Category Distribution:
Overall BMI category distribution among the 1,102 study participants 
was as follows:
- Underweight (BMI below 5th percentile): 24 children (1.5%)
- Normal weight (BMI 5th-84th percentile): 583 children (35.8%)
- Overweight (BMI 85th-94th percentile): 175 children (10.8%)
- Obese (BMI at or above 95th percentile): 320 children (19.7%)

It is notable that 525 children (32.3%) had missing BMI category data 
and were excluded from the final analysis following standard 
epidemiological practice for missing outcome data.

Obesity Prevalence by Race and Ethnicity:
Obesity prevalence varied significantly across racial and ethnic groups. 
Non-Hispanic Black children demonstrated the highest obesity prevalence 
at 21.3% (n=120 of 563), followed by Other Hispanic children at 19.9% 
(n=106 of 534), and Mexican American children at 17.7% (n=94 of 530). 
This pattern is consistent with previously published national estimates 
and supports the validity and representativeness of our study sample.

Combined overweight and obesity prevalence was highest among 
Non-Hispanic Black children (34.1%), followed by Other Hispanic 
children (30.7%) and Mexican American children (28.5%), highlighting 
the substantial burden of excess weight across all three groups.

Machine Learning Model Performance:
Two machine learning models were developed and evaluated on the 
held-out test set comprising 221 children (20% of total sample).


Random Forest Classifier achieved an overall accuracy of 88.2%. 
Performance metrics for obesity classification specifically were: 
precision 0.93, recall 0.98, and F1-score 0.96. Overweight 
classification showed moderate performance with F1-score of 0.68, 
likely reflecting the clinical overlap between overweight and obese 
categories. Underweight classification was limited by the very small 
sample size in the test set (n=3).

XGBoost Classifier demonstrated superior overall performance with 
an accuracy of 92.8%, representing a 4.6 percentage point improvement 
over Random Forest. Obesity classification performance was excellent 
with precision of 0.97, recall of 0.98, and F1-score of 0.98. 
Overweight classification also improved substantially with an 
F1-score of 0.82 compared to 0.68 for Random Forest.

Feature Importance Analysis:
Feature importance analysis using the Random Forest model identified 
the following top predictors of obesity classification in our study 
population. Anthropometric measures dominated the importance rankings, 
with BMI value accounting for 41.9% of model importance and waist 
circumference contributing 21.7%, together accounting for 63.6% of 
total feature importance. Age was the third most important predictor 
at 14.5%, reflecting the significant changes in obesity risk across 
different developmental stages of childhood and adolescence.

Among socioeconomic and behavioral factors, income to poverty ratio 
ranked fourth at 4.5%, confirming the significant role of 
socioeconomic disadvantage in obesity risk. Physical activity measures 
ranked fifth and sixth, with PE class attendance contributing 3.2% 
and daily physical activity levels contributing 2.9% of model 
importance respectively. Fast food consumption ranked eighth at 1.5% 
and soda consumption ranked fifteenth at 0.6%.

Notably race and ethnicity ranked ninth at 1.4% of feature importance, 
suggesting that after controlling for anthropometric, socioeconomic and 
behavioral factors, race itself contributes relatively little additional 
predictive value for obesity classification in this population.
